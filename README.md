## Project Background

Car theft continues to pose a significant security and economic challenge across India, particularly in urban and semi-urban areas with high vehicle density. In recent years, law enforcement agencies and insurance providers have collected detailed incident-level data capturing theft locations, vehicle characteristics, recovery outcomes, and investigative actions. However, short-term datasets are often under-analyzed despite their value in identifying immediate risks, emerging patterns, and operational gaps.

This project analyzes reported car theft cases in India recorded during the 2023 calendar year and January 2024. The dataset provides a focused, near-term view of theft activity, enabling the identification of recent patterns related to theft timing, geographic concentration, recovery performance, repeat incidents, and the effectiveness of security measures such as GPS and CCTV. The analysis emphasizes practical insights that can support short-term interventions and operational decision-making.

## Objectives

The objective of this project is to analyze reported car theft cases in India during 2023 and January 2024 to identify recent theft patterns, high-risk locations and time periods, evaluate vehicle recovery performance, assess the effectiveness of security measures such as GPS and CCTV, examine the prevalence of repeat theft incidents, and measure investigative effectiveness through suspect identification outcomes. The analysis aims to generate actionable insights that support data-driven crime prevention, improved recovery processes, and enhanced investigative decision-making.

## Insights

-  Toyota vehicles are the most targeted, recording the highest number of reported theft cases during the period under review.

-  Fuel type does not influence theft risk, as no significant preference was observed between petrol, diesel, or other fuel categories.

-  Vehicle thefts occur most frequently in the evening, indicating higher risk during late-day and early-night hours.

-  Vehicle recovery performance is low, with only 30% of stolen cars successfully recovered.

-  Repeat theft is a major concern, as 67% of cases involve vehicles that had been stolen previously, highlighting weak post-theft deterrence.

-  GPS adoption is limited, with 60% of stolen vehicles lacking GPS tracking systems, reducing the likelihood of recovery.

-  Investigative effectiveness is low, as suspects were identified in only 20% of reported cases.

-  Recovery is slow, with an average recovery time of 18 days for stolen vehicles, suggesting delays in investigation and tracking.

## Recommendation

1.  **Target high-risk vehicle brands**
Law enforcement and vehicle owners should prioritize theft prevention strategies for high-risk brands such as Toyota, including enhanced surveillance, owner awareness campaigns, and manufacturer-supported security features.

2.  **Strengthen evening and night patrols**
Since thefts peak in the evening, deploying additional patrols, checkpoints, and surveillance during these hours could significantly reduce incidents.

3.  **Promote GPS installation and usage**
Increased adoption of GPS tracking systems should be encouraged through insurance incentives, regulatory policies, or manufacturer partnerships, as GPS absence is strongly linked to low recovery rates.

4.  **Improve post-recovery security for repeat victims**
Given the high repeat theft rate, recovered vehicles should undergo mandatory or strongly recommended security upgrades (GPS, immobilizers, CCTV coverage at parking locations).

5.  **Enhance CCTV coverage and usability**
Expanding CCTV coverage in high-theft locations and improving footage quality can support faster suspect identification and improve investigative outcomes.

6.  **Accelerate investigation and recovery timelines**
Reducing the average recovery time of 18 days should be a priority by improving inter-agency coordination, data sharing, and real-time tracking mechanisms.

7.  **Focus on investigative capacity building**
The low suspect identification rate suggests a need for improved forensic analysis, digital evidence handling, and technology-driven policing.

## Conclusion

The results of this study indicate that car theft in India during the period under review is characterized by high repeat victimization, low recovery rates, and limited investigative success. The prevalence of repeat thefts suggests that existing deterrence measures are insufficient, particularly after an initial theft occurs. Limited adoption of tracking technologies and inadequate surveillance infrastructure further reduce the likelihood of timely recovery and suspect identification.

To effectively address these challenges, a shift toward technology-driven prevention, targeted policing during high-risk periods, and improved investigative processes is essential. Strengthening GPS and CCTV adoption, prioritizing high-risk vehicle categories, and accelerating recovery timelines can significantly improve outcomes. While the dataset provides valuable short-term insights, sustained data collection and enhanced data granularity will be critical for tracking long-term trends and evaluating the impact of future interventions.
